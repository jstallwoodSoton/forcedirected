A Glimpse into the Wonderful World of Force-Directed Graphs

for Lily, from James

We begin with a graph:

$$ G = \{\{a,b,c,d,e,f,g\},\{ab, ac, ad, bf, eg\}\} $$

Each vertex, \(v\) in \(V(G)\), can be drawn on a plane. Let \(p(v)\) be the position of the vertex. At the start, the position of each vertex will be random.

Our goal is to have adjacent vertices attract each other and non-adjacent vertices repel each other. That way the vertices will naturally spread out into a pleasing aesthetic shape.

To do this we need some ideal length, \(l\).

What we shall do is set up a counter, \(t\), and for every step such that \(t \lt K, K \in \mathbb{N}\), the vertices will attract and repel each other as appropriate.

  • \(t \leftarrow 0\)
  • WHILE \(t \lt K\):
    • \(F_{a}(v) \leftarrow \sum\limits_{w \in \Gamma(v)} 2\ln\left(\frac{||p(v) - p(w)||}{l}\right)\overrightarrow{p(w)p(v)}\)
    • \(F_{r}(v) \leftarrow \sum\limits_{w \in V(G)\setminus\Gamma(v)} \left(\frac{l}{||p(v) - p(w)||^{2}}\right)\overrightarrow{p(v)p(w)}\)
    • \(p(v) \leftarrow p(v) + \alpha(F_{a}(v) + F_{r}(v)), \forall v \in V(G)\)
    • \(t \leftarrow t + 1\)

Where \(F_{a}(v)\) is the attractive force, \(F_{r}(v)\) is the repelling force, and \(\alpha\) is some cooling constant, usually around 0.1
